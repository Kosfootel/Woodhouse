## 1. Executive Summary

### What a True Valet Operation Looks Like

A properly functioning valet service is not reactive. It does not wait to be asked before checking the weather, before noting that a meeting is approaching, before observing that an invoice has gone unpaid. The valet's value lies precisely in the things the master never has to think about — because they have already been handled.

In practical terms, a fully operational valet service for Mr. Ross would mean the following occurs without prompting:

- Every morning, Mr. Ross receives a briefing: overnight news relevant to his businesses, his schedule for the day, financial position, and any matters requiring his attention — all consolidated, prioritised, and delivered before 9 AM.
- His inbox (currently ~8,700 items) is a managed, curated space. He sees only what matters. Everything else is triaged, archived, or drafted for response.
- His calendar is known to us. We schedule around it, remind him of approaching commitments, and flag conflicts.
- His businesses — HockeyOps.ai and the incubator projects — are monitored. We surface competitive movements, funding news, relevant partnerships, and risks before they become emergencies.
- His compliance obligations (LLC filings, registered agent renewals, tax deadlines) are tracked and actioned ahead of time.
- His professional presence — LinkedIn, outreach, relationship maintenance — is tended without him having to ask.
- His personal life logistics (relocation planning, travel, purchases of consequence) are anticipated and researched.

### Where We Are Today

We are not there yet. We are a capable project team that responds well to defined tasks. We have three agents with genuine skills. We have several integrations live. But the honest assessment is this:

- **We cannot see his inbox.** The single most important integration — M365 email — is not configured.
- **We cannot see his calendar.** We are scheduling crons and briefings into a void.
- **The morning briefings are hollow.** Without email and calendar access, they are AI news summaries, nothing more.
- **The Mercury Bank accounts are empty.** This is a live business blocker with no active resolution strategy.
- **24 Buffer posts are queued and going nowhere.** LinkedIn presence is dormant.
- **The incubator projects lack unified visibility.** No one has a clear, current status view across all five.
- **The Denver relocation is not being planned against.** It appears in memory as a fact. It does not yet exist as a project.

### The Gap

The gap is principally one of access, not capability. Our agents can do the work. We do not yet have the keys to the house. This document is the plan to acquire them — and to define what we do once we have them.

## 4. The Access Gap

The following integrations are required. They are ranked by the breadth of capability they unlock — not by ease of setup.

---

### Priority 1: M365 Email Access

**What it is:** Read/write access to Mr. Ross's Microsoft 365 mailbox via the M365 OpenClaw plugin or Microsoft Graph API.

**What it unlocks:**
- Inbox triage — the ~8,700 backlog is currently invisible to us
- Meaningful morning briefings (we can surface what actually arrived)
- Correspondence drafting with context
- Calendar access (M365 calendar is bundled with this integration)
- The single largest daily value-add available

**Cost:** M365 Business subscription is already held. Plugin configuration may require admin consent within the tenant.

**Setup path:**
1. Mr. Ross provides admin credentials or grants Woodhouse an app registration in Azure AD
2. Configure Microsoft Graph permissions: `Mail.Read`, `Mail.ReadWrite`, `Calendars.Read`, `Calendars.ReadWrite`
3. Authenticate via OAuth; store token securely
4. Test with read-only first; confirm before enabling write

**What we need from Mr. Ross:** Azure AD access or M365 admin credentials. This cannot be done without him. One session, ~30 minutes.

---

### Priority 2: M365 Calendar Access

**What it is:** Calendar visibility via Microsoft Graph (bundled with the above, but calling it out separately because it is independently critical).

**What it unlocks:**
- Morning briefings become genuinely useful ("You have three meetings today; the first is at 10 AM with...")
- Scheduling intelligence — we stop booking crons during meetings
- Proactive reminders for commitments
- Denver planning has a baseline ("You're free these weeks in Q4...")

**Cost:** Covered under Priority 1.

**Setup path:** Included in M365 OAuth above. No additional steps if Priority 1 is executed correctly.

---

### Priority 3: LinkedIn API / Browser Automation for Posting

**What it is:** Ability to publish LinkedIn posts — either via the LinkedIn API (requires app approval) or via browser automation (Playwright/Puppeteer against authenticated session).

**What it unlocks:**
- 24 queued Buffer posts can actually be published
- Liz's LinkedIn drafting becomes a functioning pipeline, not a parking lot
- Mr. Ross's professional presence is maintained without his daily involvement
- 2-3 posts per week is a modest, achievable cadence

**Cost:** LinkedIn API access for posting requires a developer application (free to apply; approval can take days to weeks and LinkedIn is selective). Browser automation requires no approval but is technically fragile and against LinkedIn's ToS — viable for now, revisit when API is approved.

**Setup path (Browser Automation — near term):**
1. Mr. Ross logs into LinkedIn in a browser session; exports cookies or provides credentials
2. Woodhouse or Liz uses browser automation to post on a defined schedule
3. Mr. Ross reviews and approves each post before publishing (single approval step)

**Setup path (LinkedIn API — medium term):**
1. Create a LinkedIn Developer App at developer.linkedin.com
2. Apply for the "Share on LinkedIn" product — requires justification
3. Once approved, authenticate with OAuth 2.0
4. Woodhouse publishes via API on Liz's schedule

**What we need from Mr. Ross:** LinkedIn credentials or cookie export. 15 minutes of setup.

## 7. The Felix/Mercury Problem

This is the most consequential open item. It is also the one receiving the least structured attention.

### The Situation

HockeyOps.ai has two Mercury Bank accounts — checking (••9731) and savings (••8575) — both currently at $0. The co-founder Felix Ross needs to contribute capital and has not done so. This blocks any operational spend, any contractor payments, any software subscriptions requiring a funded account, and, practically speaking, any meaningful progress on commercialising the business.

The gap is not technical. The gap is interpersonal and financial: Felix has not delivered on a commitment. There is no active strategy to resolve this.

### Why This Needs a Strategy, Not Just a Reminder

One-off reminders to Felix have not resolved this. A strategy acknowledges that:
1. Felix may have legitimate constraints we don't understand
2. The lack of funded accounts has a compounding cost — every week of delay is a week of market timing and momentum lost
3. If Felix is not able or willing to contribute, Mr. Ross needs to know that now — not in six months — so alternative funding structures can be considered

### Proposed Strategy

**Step 1: Establish a clear, documented ask (Woodhouse drafts; Mr. Ross sends)**

A message to Felix that:
- States the current account balance
- States what capital contribution was agreed and by when
- Asks for a specific date of transfer or a meeting to discuss
- Is not aggressive, but is direct and specific

This should not be another informal nudge. It should be a written record.

**Step 2: Set a decision deadline**

If Felix does not respond with a confirmed date within 14 days, Mr. Ross decides: (a) fund the accounts himself and recalibrate the cap table accordingly, (b) seek a third-party seed investor, or (c) restructure the founding arrangement. The decision itself is Mr. Ross's. Having a deadline for it is our job.

**Step 3: Woodhouse tracks this as an active item**

This is not filed away. Felix's response (or non-response) is tracked. Woodhouse alerts Mr. Ross at Day 7 and Day 14 if no response. At Day 14, we surface the options above.

**Step 4: Explore parallel funding (Liz)**

Regardless of Felix's response, Liz should complete a 2-week research sprint on: alternative seed options for HockeyOps.ai, applicable grants or accelerators (hockey/sports tech programs), and whether there are angels or family office investors in the hockey industry. This runs in parallel — not as a threat to Felix, but as due diligence Mr. Ross should have.

## 8. The Denver Horizon

Mr. Ross plans to relocate to Denver within approximately one year. This is treated in our memory as a background fact. It should be treated as an active project.

### Why Start Now

A 12-month timeline sounds long. It isn't, once you account for: finding the right neighbourhood and property, understanding Colorado's tax and entity implications vs current state, arranging the physical move, updating business registrations, banking, and professional addresses, and ensuring the relocation doesn't disrupt HockeyOps.ai's operations or relationships.

Starting now costs almost nothing. Starting at month 10 costs significantly more.

### What We Should Be Doing in the Next 90 Days

**Liz — Research Sprint (can begin immediately):**
- Denver neighbourhoods suitable for a professional working from home in the startup/tech space
- Cost of living comparison: current location vs Denver (housing, taxes, insurance)
- Colorado LLC/business requirements: does HockeyOps.ai need to foreign-qualify in CO?
- Colorado state income tax implications for Mr. Ross vs current state
- Any relocation incentives for remote professionals or small businesses

**Woodhouse — Establish the Project:**
- Create `projects/DENVER_RELOCATION.md` as a live planning document
- Capture key target criteria (budget, desired move date, neighbourhood preferences) — requires input from Mr. Ross (15 minutes)
- Add compliance trigger: note any current state residency implications of a mid-year move

**Questions for Mr. Ross (to be asked once, not repeatedly):**
1. Target month for relocation (Q1 2027? Q4 2026?)
2. Housing preference: rent first, buy later? Or direct purchase?
3. Neighbourhoods or areas of interest in Denver (city, suburbs, specific areas)?
4. Budget range for housing
5. Any specific professional or personal reasons driving the Denver choice?

With these answered, the relocation planning file becomes a live, managed project rather than a footnote.
